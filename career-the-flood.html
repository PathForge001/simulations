Scene 1

The River
is Rising

Could you help save your town?

It has been raining for three days. Not normal rain — the kind that turns the sky grey and makes rivers churn brown.

You live near the River Tyne. This morning your dad pointed at the water level marker on the old bridge. The red line. The water is nearly touching it.

At school, your teacher says the town council are meeting tonight to decide whether to build a new flood barrier. But they need information first — and nobody has measured how fast the river is rising.

Your teacher looks at the class.

"The council needs someone to go and measure the water level every hour today. They need three measurements. Who wants to help?"

Your hand goes up before you even think about it.

🔬
Real Science Fact!
Environmental scientists measure things called "variables" — numbers that change over time, like river water levels. By recording the change, they can predict what happens next. That is called data collection!

You walk to the bridge with your notebook. The water is 2.3 metres. You write it down carefully.

One hour later: 2.5 metres. Another hour: 2.8 metres.

The river is rising 0.25 metres every hour.

You run back to school. The council meeting is in two hours. Your teacher asks you to present your findings. But there is a problem — she wants you to also tell the council what you think they should do.

What do you recommend?

Scene 2 — The Next Morning

The Big Question

The council used your data. Some houses were protected. The river peaked at 3.1 metres and then slowly went back down. Nobody got flooded.

Your town is safe. And everyone at school knows it was partly because of you.

Your teacher sits down with you. She says: "You know what you did yesterday? That is what environmental scientists do every single day."

🌍
Did You Know?
Environmental scientists study rivers, air, soil, and weather. Some work for governments. Some work for charities. Some travel the world measuring glaciers and rainforests. They help humans live alongside nature without destroying it.

She shows you three different jobs that exist because of what you did yesterday:

A hydrologist — a scientist who studies rivers and predicts floods.
A civil engineer — someone who designs barriers, bridges, and flood defences.
An emergency planner — someone who decides what to do when disasters happen.

She asks you: "Which one sounds most like you?"

Which path interests you most?
