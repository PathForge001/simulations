The Water Detective

Gitanjali Rao · Age 10 · STEM School Highlands Ranch

Gitanjali Rao is ten years old, watching the news with her parents. A city called Flint, Michigan has been drinking water full of lead for years — invisible, tasteless, and causing brain damage in children her age.

Then one evening she watches her own parents dip test strips into their tap water. The results are vague. Inconclusive. Are they safe? They do not actually know.

"This is not a reliable process," she thinks. "And I've got to do something to change this." She is ten. No lab, no funding, no degree. What she has is a question that will not leave her alone.

The StoryThe Idea · 2016
Late 2016 · Gitanjali's bedroom

The MIT Website

Where the idea came from

Weeks of reading later, Gitanjali finds something on the MIT research website: carbon nanotubes — microscopic tubes that detect hazardous gases by changing their electrical resistance when molecules attach to them.

She reads it twice. Then she asks the question nobody else has asked: why can't this work for water?

🔬
The science idea
If lead molecules attach to treated nanotubes the same way gas molecules do, a tiny sensor could test water in seconds — for a few dollars, not a few weeks.

She has the idea. But she is ten, her family has just moved cross-country to Colorado, she knows nobody at her new school — and the chemicals involved are hazardous. She cannot do this in her bedroom.

The Final Presentation

Gitanjali Rao · Age 11 · The Young Scientist Challenge Final

Eleven months have passed. Gitanjali has a working prototype — a small blue 3D-printed device the size of a deck of cards. She named it Tethys, after the Greek goddess of fresh water.

Tethys has a carbon nanotube sensor specially treated to react to lead. When dipped in contaminated water, the lead molecules attach to the nanotubes and change the electrical resistance. A Bluetooth chip sends the reading to a smartphone app that Gitanjali designed with her computer science teacher. The result appears in seconds. The prototype cost just over $20 to make.

She is now one of ten finalists at the Discovery Education 3M Young Scientist Challenge — the youngest finalist in the room. The judges are professional scientists and education administrators from across the country.

She has five minutes to present. Everything she has worked on for the past year comes down to this. But there is one more decision to make.

Gitanjali's mentor at 3M, Kathleen Shafer, spent weeks helping her condense the presentation down to five minutes. She told Gitanjali: "Show them what it does. Make them feel the problem before you show them the solution."

Gitanjali is standing backstage. In two minutes she walks on. How does she open her five-minute presentation?

What happened next

Gitanjali won the 2017 Discovery Education 3M Young Scientist Challenge and was awarded $25,000 — which she immediately put back into developing Tethys further. In 2019 she appeared on Forbes 30 Under 30. In 2020, TIME named her their first-ever Kid of the Year. She received a US patent for Tethys in 2021. She has since run innovation workshops for more than 40,000 young people across four continents. In 2025 she received the first Starmus Stephen Hawking Junior Medal for Science Communications. She is currently studying biological engineering at MIT. She started all of this at age ten because she could not sleep after watching the news.

"I always tell everybody who wants to create a device but is worried it won't work — do not be afraid, to try. Failure is just another step to success." — Gitanjali Rao, age 11
