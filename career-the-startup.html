Scene 1 · The Decision

The Offer

Six months of work. One decision. No right answer.

You and your two friends — Kai and Priya — built something. A platform that connects local freelance tutors with families in your area. You built it in six months, working evenings and weekends.

It is not much. But it works. 47 tutors signed up. 180 families use it. £12,000 in transaction fees processed last month.

EduGroup — Acquisition Enquiry
"We have been tracking TutorLink with interest. We would like to discuss a potential acquisition of the platform and technology for £85,000. We believe this would be beneficial for both parties. Would you be available for a call this week?"

£85,000. Split three ways. That is £28,000 each.

Kai says take it immediately. Priya says wait — they can build something much bigger. You are the tiebreaker.

— What do you do first? —

Scene 2 · Three Months Later

The Crossroads

Three months have passed. Whatever happened with EduGroup, you are still building.

TutorLink has grown: 140 tutors, 520 families, £38,000 in monthly transactions. You have applied to a startup accelerator called Emerge Education, which funds and mentors early-stage edtech companies.

They accept you. The offer: £50,000 in funding in exchange for 8% equity in TutorLink.

But the accelerator comes with a condition. They want you to focus on London only — build deeply in one market before expanding. Kai wants to expand nationally immediately. He says the London-only strategy is too slow and you will miss the window.

Priya says: "We should take the money and follow the accelerator's advice. They have done this before. We haven't."

Kai says: "We are growing 60% month-on-month. We do not need their advice. We need their money — then we do what we think is right."

The team is split. You are the deciding vote again.

— What do you decide? —

Now think it through.

Founders, investors, product managers, and financial analysts make decisions like these every day. There is never a right answer — only better reasoning.

Question 01
£85,000 is a lot of money. £28,000 each is life-changing at 17. Why is it still not always the right answer to take it?
Question 02
Kai and Priya disagree on almost every decision. What is the difference between a founding team disagreement that makes a company stronger and one that destroys it?
Question 03
TutorLink helps tutors earn money and families access education. What responsibility does that create — and how does it change the decisions you can make?
