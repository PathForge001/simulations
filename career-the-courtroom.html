What do you do when the system gets it wrong?

Your school runs a mock trial. You have been assigned to defend a student called Omar, who is accused of something he says he did not do.

The charge: Omar allegedly vandalised the school library — spray-painted a wall. The evidence against him: one witness who says they saw him, and a can of paint found near his locker.

You know Omar. He is quiet. He reads more than anyone in your year. He would not vandalise the library.

But knowing something and proving it are different things.

Scene 2 · The Courtroom

The Cross-Examination

The trial begins. The prosecution presents their case. Jade gives her witness statement confidently: she saw someone in a red hoodie near the library at 4pm. Omar owns a red hoodie.

The prosecution rests. Now it is your turn.

You stand up. The panel of three teachers who are acting as the jury look at you.

Witness Statement — Jade T., for the Prosecution
"I was walking past the library at approximately 4pm on Tuesday. I saw a person in a red hoodie crouching by the side wall. I did not see their face clearly but I recognised their posture and build. I am fairly confident it was Omar."

"Fairly confident."

You notice that phrase. You circle it in your notes.

You have one question you can ask her. What is it?

Think like a lawyer.

Lawyers, judges, policy makers, and investigators all use the thinking you just practised. Take a moment to examine what you did.

Question 01
What is the difference between believing someone is guilty and being able to prove it? Why does that difference matter?
Question 02
Jade said "fairly confident." In a criminal trial, that phrase could set someone free. Why are words so important in law?
Question 03
Think of a situation you know about — in school, in the news, in your community — where you think justice was not done. What would it take to change that?
