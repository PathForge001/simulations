Security Operations Centre · 3:47am

The Breach

3:47am. An alert just lit up. Something is inside the network.

You are a cybersecurity analyst on the night shift at a hospital trust's Security Operations Centre. The screens glow quietly. Most nights, nothing happens.

Then at 3:47am, an alert flares red. Unusual data is moving out of the network — large files, encrypted, heading to an address nobody recognises. Someone, or something, is inside.

This is a hospital. Patient records. Appointment systems. Equipment that keeps people alive. And you are the only analyst awake.

ProgressScene 1

The alert could be a real attack — or a false alarm from a routine backup. But if it is real, every minute you wait is more data stolen, or worse, systems locked by ransomware.

⏱️
What you know
Disconnecting the affected server would stop the data leak instantly — but if it is running a live system, you could knock out something a ward depends on. You have minutes, not hours.

Act too slowly and the breach spreads. Act too rashly and you cause the outage yourself.

The immediate threat is contained. Now the harder part begins — because a breach is never just a technical problem. It is people, trust, and consequences.

Your investigation reveals how they got in: a nurse clicked a link in a convincing fake email three days ago, handing over a password without realising. The attacker walked in through that door.

💬
The human question
The nurse did nothing malicious — she was busy, the email looked real, and anyone could have fallen for it. Some patient data was accessed before you stopped it. This now has to be handled — technically, legally, and humanely.
