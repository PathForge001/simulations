Opening

The Algorithm

You built it. Now it's doing something you didn't intend.

You are six months into your first job. Junior product manager at a mid-size UK fintech company called Clerk. Your team built a credit-scoring algorithm — an AI system that decides whether people qualify for small business loans.

The algorithm has been live for three months. It is performing well by every metric your manager tracks. Approval rate. Default rate. Processing speed. Everything looks clean.

Then last Tuesday, a junior data analyst on your team — Priya — sends you a private Slack message at 6:43pm. She has been running the numbers in her own time.

// slack · private · priya.mehta → you · 18:43
"hey. don't want to send this in the main channel.
i've been looking at the approval data broken down by postcode.
applicants from SE1, E1, N16 have a 34% lower approval rate
than applicants with identical financial profiles from SW3, W1."
"i think the algorithm is discriminating by area.
which means it's effectively discriminating by race.
this might be illegal. what do i do with this?"

You sit with that message for a long time. The algorithm is yours. You helped design it. It went through compliance review and nobody flagged anything. Your manager is presenting the Q3 performance results to the board in four days.

You are 22 years old. This is your first real job. Priya is asking you what to do.

You are the one who has to decide whether to pull this thread or leave it alone.

— What do you do? —

Scene 2 · The Pressure

The Meeting

Three days have passed. Your manager — Sarah — has called you into a room. She has seen the analysis. She closes the door.

She says the company's legal team has confirmed it: the postcode correlation does constitute indirect discrimination under the Equality Act 2010. The algorithm needs to be suspended immediately or significantly modified.

Then she says something that catches you off guard.

"The board presentation is tomorrow. If we suspend the algorithm today it becomes a story. The press will find out. Investors will panic. This could seriously damage the company."

"I need you to help me think about how we handle the timing of this. We could present tomorrow as planned, suspend the algorithm quietly next week, and fix it before anyone gets hurt. One more week. Nobody new gets denied unfairly in that time — we would pause new applications."

She is not asking you to cover it up. She is asking you to delay the disclosure by one week. What do you do?

ProgressReflection
⚡

Think it through.

Every decision in this simulation was a real type of decision that product managers, data scientists, compliance officers, and tech founders face. Not once — regularly.

Question 01
Priya found the problem in her own time and sent you a private message rather than posting it publicly. What does that tell you about the culture she was working in — and what would a healthy culture have looked like instead?
Question 02
Sarah was not asking you to lie. She was asking you to delay by one week. Where is the line between practical timing and compromising your integrity — and how do you know when you have crossed it?
Question 03
The algorithm discriminated by postcode, not by race. But the effect was the same. What does that tell you about the gap between what a system is designed to do and what it actually does — and whose responsibility is it to close that gap?
