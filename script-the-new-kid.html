Opening

The
New Kid

Making a friend in a world where everyone just used to follow each other instead.

There is a new girl in your year called Amara. She started three weeks ago, right when the ban came in.

That timing matters. Before the ban, a new kid could ease into a school slowly — follow a few people, get followed back, see what people were into. The digital version of belonging before the real version.

Amara does not have that. She came in cold.

You watch her in the lunch hall on a Wednesday. She is sitting at the edge of a table, not quite with the group and not quite alone. She is reading a book about architecture. She has drawn something in the margin. You notice it is actually quite good.

What you observe · Wednesday lunchtime
She laughed once at something in the book. Then looked up to see if anyone noticed. Nobody did. She went back to reading.

You have your own friends. You are fine. You do not need to do anything.

But you noticed. And now you cannot un-notice.

The old way to connect with someone new would have been to follow them online — a low-risk, low-effort signal that says "I see you."

There is no low-risk option anymore. Whatever you do next, you will have to actually do it.

— What do you do? —

Scene 2

The Project

Two weeks later. Your school announces a community project — groups of four students will design something for a local space. A mural, a garden, a pop-up library, a community noticeboard. Real brief. Real location. Real presentation to the council at the end.

Your teacher assigns the groups. You and Amara are in the same one, along with two others — Marcus (yes, that Marcus) and a girl called Rosa who is incredibly organised but very quiet.

The project requires your group to make decisions together. The first one is the hardest: what do you build?

Amara immediately suggests a community noticeboard — physical, in the town square, where local people can pin things. Events, lost pets, things to give away, offers of help.

"Like a social media feed, but real," she says. Then laughs. "Too soon?"

Marcus wants to do a mural. Rosa wants to do a garden. You have not said what you want yet.

The group is stuck. Everyone has an idea. Nobody wants to be the one who kills someone else's.

You are the one who knows all three of them best. What do you do?

— How do you move the group forward? —

ProgressReflection
🌱

Slow down and think.

Youth workers, community organisers, and psychologists spend their lives helping groups of people who do not quite fit together yet — find a way to work together. You just did that.

Question 01
Amara arrived in a world without the digital shortcut to belonging. What do you think that actually felt like — and what did you notice about her that made you want to help?
Question 02
When the group was stuck, you had to find a way through. What does it take to move a group of people who all have different ideas — without making anyone feel dismissed?
Question 03
Amara described the noticeboard as "social media but real." What do you think is lost when everything moves online — and what comes back when it is taken away?

You helped someone belong. Then you helped a group of people with different ideas find a way through. Both of those things are harder in person than online — and more real.

What this simulation was really testing
→
Noticing people
Amara laughed at her book and looked up to see if anyone noticed. You noticed. That is the first step in community psychology — paying attention to the person who is slightly on the edge.
→
Facilitation
Moving a group from stuck to moving is one of the most valuable skills in youth work, community organising, teaching, and management. It requires you to care more about the group's outcome than about being right.
→
The difference between belonging and being present
Amara was physically at the table. She did not belong yet. Belonging is something given by other people — which means it can be given deliberately, not just accidentally.
→
Community design
The noticeboard idea — a physical space for local connection — is what urban planners, community architects, and social designers actually work on. Real jobs, built on exactly these instincts.
