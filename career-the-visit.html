Home care round · 8:10am

The Visit

You have fifteen minutes booked. Mr Adeyemi needs more than that.

You are a home care worker. Your job is to visit older and disabled people in their own homes — helping them wash, dress, take medication, and eat. You have eleven visits booked today, each one timed to the minute.

Your third visit is Mr Adeyemi, 82, who lives alone since his wife died last year. You are booked for fifteen minutes: help him with breakfast and his tablets, then move on.

But when you arrive, something is wrong. His curtains are still shut. He is sitting in his chair, still in yesterday's clothes, and he has not eaten. He looks at you and quietly says he did not see the point in getting up.

ProgressScene 1

This is not a medical emergency you can point to. Mr Adeyemi is clean, safe, and physically fine. But he is clearly low — perhaps very low — and lonely in a way that fifteen minutes cannot fix.

⏱️
The pressure you're under
You have eight more people to visit, several of whom need you at specific times for medication. If you stay longer with Mr Adeyemi, someone later in your round gets a rushed or late visit. Your schedule has no slack in it.

The task on your sheet is breakfast and tablets. The person in front of you needs something the sheet does not mention.

Whatever you did that morning, Mr Adeyemi stayed on your mind. Over the next few weeks you notice a pattern: several of the older people on your round are desperately isolated. The care system keeps them clean, fed, and safe — but not connected.

At a team meeting, your supervisor asks if anyone has ideas for improving care quality. You have been thinking about exactly this.

💡
Your chance to change something
You are just one carer, new-ish, not senior. But you have seen something real that the people who design the schedules may not see: the fifteen-minute visit keeps bodies alive while leaving people profoundly alone.
