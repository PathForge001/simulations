Opening

The Group Chat
is Gone

What happens when the way you talk to each other disappears overnight.

It has been three days since the ban came in.

Three days since the government switched off under-16 access to TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat, and everything else. Schools are calling it a "digital detox." Parents are mostly relieved. Your friends are mostly furious.

You are at school on Friday morning when you notice something. Marcus hasn't shown up.

Before the ban, you would have already seen fifteen stories from him this morning. Now there is nothing. No way to check. No one to DM. The group chat you have all used every single day for two years — gone. Just like that.

You ask around. Nobody has seen Marcus. His form tutor looks slightly worried but says nothing.

You know Marcus has been having a rough time at home lately. He told you once — just once — that things were bad. You said "that sounds hard" and then someone posted a meme and the conversation moved on.

You have been thinking about that conversation for three days.

Now you have no way to reach him except to actually do something.

Scene 2

The Article

Marcus turns up at lunch. He is fine — he had a dentist appointment and his mum forgot to ring school. You feel simultaneously relieved and slightly embarrassed about how worried you were.

But the morning has left something in you. Walking the corridors, you noticed how different school felt. Groups of people actually talking. Some looking lost. One girl was crying in the bathroom, and when her friend asked what was wrong she said: "I don't know what to do with myself."

Your media studies teacher sets an assignment: "Write about something real that is happening in your community right now."

You have been thinking about the ban all week. About what it has changed. About what it has revealed.

You could write something safe. Or you could write something honest — about what it actually feels like to lose the one place where you could be yourself, 24 hours a day.

But honest might mean saying things some people will not want to read. It might mean quoting people. It might mean arguing something controversial.

What kind of story do you write?

Think like a journalist.

Journalists make decisions every day about what to say, how to say it, and whose voice gets heard. The choices you made in this simulation are the same ones they face.

Question 01
When you realised Marcus was missing, what was the first thing you felt — and did that feeling lead to a good decision?
Question 02
The ban took away one way of communicating. What did it force you to use instead — and was that actually worse, or just different?
Question 03
If you were a journalist writing about the social media ban, whose voice would you most want people to hear — and why is that voice usually left out?

What this simulation was really testing
→
Real-world connection without digital tools
When the platform disappears, the only option is to actually reach out. That requires more courage than a DM — and creates a different kind of connection.
→
Editorial judgement
Safe stories protect the writer. Honest stories serve the reader. Journalists have to decide which one they are writing — and live with the consequences of that choice.
→
Empathy as information
Noticing that the girl in the bathroom said "I don't know what to do with myself" is data. Journalists who pay attention to the people around them find the stories others miss.
→
Voice and power
In every debate about young people, young people's voices are the last ones heard. What does it mean to change that — and what does it cost to try?
