Cleveland, 1923

The Crash at the Crossing

He saw the accident happen. He knew he could stop the next one.

Garrett Morgan was born in 1877 in Kentucky, the son of formerly enslaved parents. He left school after the sixth grade to find work. But Garrett had a gift — he could look at a machine and see how to make it better.

By 1923 he has already invented a breathing device — a "safety hood" that lets people breathe in smoke and toxic air. He once used it himself to rescue men trapped in a collapsed tunnel filling with poison gas. But when fire departments in the South learned the inventor was a Black man, many cancelled their orders.

Now Garrett is stopped at a Cleveland intersection when he sees it happen — a car slams into a horse-drawn carriage. The signals of the day only said STOP and GO. There was no warning. No moment to clear the crossing. He watches the wreck and thinks: this will keep happening until someone fixes the signal itself.

ProgressScene 1

Garrett has an idea: a signal with a third position — a warning between STOP and GO, so the crossing can clear before traffic moves. It could save countless lives.

⚙️
What he is weighing
Garrett already runs several businesses and has patents. But developing and patenting a new invention costs money and time — and he knows that being a Black inventor in 1923 means his ideas are often dismissed or stolen outright.

He has the idea and the skill. The question is how to bring it into the world in a way that actually reaches people — and survives.

What happened next
Garrett Morgan sold his traffic signal patent to General Electric for $40,000. His three-position design became the foundation of the modern traffic light used around the world. His earlier "safety hood" was a forerunner of the gas masks that saved soldiers' lives in World War I. He spent his life inventing, running businesses, and funding Black education and civil rights — a man with a sixth-grade education whose ideas still protect you every single day.

Your journey through Garrett's decision

What made this possible
Primary factor: Boldness. Garrett had already been burned once for inventing while Black — fire departments cancelled orders for his life-saving safety hood the moment they learned who made it. He built the traffic signal anyway, and patented it under his own name anyway, refusing to let prejudice decide what he was allowed to create.
Secondary factor: Market understanding. When General Electric offered to buy the patent, Garrett chose the scale of impact over the safety of staying small and unnoticed — understanding that a national company could put his invention on corners he would never personally reach.
His own core value: Building anyway. Garrett never let one rejection become the final answer. He kept inventing, kept patenting, kept trying — treating every closed door as information, not as a verdict on his worth.
