The Missing
Post

A story about what you do when someone goes quiet.

It's a Tuesday night. You're doing homework with your phone face-down — then you pick it up for a second, just to check.

You scroll past the usual stuff. Then something stops you.

👤
Jay · your closest friend
Posted 11 minutes ago
"Some days it genuinely feels like nothing I do matters. Like I could disappear and the world would just... keep going. Anyway. Doesn't matter."
💬 0 comments ❤️ 3 likes 👁 14 views

Jay posted that eleven minutes ago. Three people liked it. Nobody commented.

You and Jay have been friends since year 7. They're funny and sarcastic and they never post things like this.

Your stomach tightens.

You don't know if this is serious or just a bad day. You don't know what to say. You don't even know if you should say anything.

But you're the one who noticed.

— What do you do? —

The Next Morning

You get to school. Jay isn't there.

By 10am, you've heard two different rumours. First: Jay just skipped because of an exam. Second: someone called Jay's mum last night and there was some kind of scene at their house.

Nobody knows which is true. The group chat is buzzing with speculation. People are tagging each other and saying things like "so dramatic" and "they're always like this."

Then your phone buzzes.

It's a message from Jay — sent at 7:42am, while you were on the bus.

👤
Jay
07:42 · This morning
"I know someone told my mum about the post. I get that they were trying to help. But I'm so embarrassed right now I can't even face school. I just needed someone to actually talk to me, not go around me."

You read it three times.

Jay is clearly struggling. And now they also feel exposed — like the one moment they were vulnerable was turned into a situation, not a conversation.

— What do you do now? —

Now sit with it.

People who work in psychology, counselling, and social work face moments like this every day. There's rarely a clean right answer. What matters is how you think it through.

Question 01
When you saw Jay's post, what was the first feeling that hit you — and did that feeling drive what you chose to do?
Question 02
Think about Jay on that Tuesday night, reading the reactions — or not seeing any. What do you think they needed most in that moment?
Question 03
Was there a moment in this simulation where doing the "right thing" and doing the "kind thing" felt different? What would you do if you faced this for real?

What this simulation was really testing
→
Empathy vs action
Knowing someone is hurting is one thing. Deciding what to do about it — and how — is a different skill entirely.
→
Understanding privacy and dignity
When you involve other people in someone else's pain, even with good intentions, there are consequences. Psychologists and social workers think about this every day.
→
Reading between the lines
Jay's post didn't say "I need help." It said something quieter. Noticing the quiet signals is one of the most important skills in human-facing careers.
→
Communication under emotional pressure
When you're scared of saying the wrong thing, silence can feel safe. But silence has consequences too.
