The Number That Doesn't Add Up

Everyone wants this deal to close Friday. You just found a problem.

You are a junior analyst at an investment firm. For three weeks the whole team has been working late on one thing: a £40 million deal to buy a company called Halden Logistics. It closes Friday. Everyone is exhausted and everyone is ready.

It is Thursday, 9pm. You are doing a final check of Halden's accounts — routine, box-ticking work nobody expects to find anything in. Then a number catches your eye. The company's reported profits don't match its cash.

You check again. The revenue looks strong on paper, but the actual money coming in is far lower. Something has been made to look better than it really is. And you are the most junior person in the building.

ProgressScene 1

If you are right, the company is worth far less than £40 million — maybe the whole deal is a mistake. But you are 23, it is your first job, and the senior partners have staked three weeks and their reputations on this closing tomorrow.

📉
What you're seeing
Profits and cash should move together. When reported profit is high but cash is low, it can mean revenue is being recorded that hasn't actually arrived — sometimes an honest timing issue, sometimes something worse.

You could be about to stop a £40 million mistake — or embarrass yourself over something you misread as a junior.
