Scene 1

The Missing Player

Your team's best player has vanished. The final is in three days.

You are the captain of your school football team. You have been working towards this regional final for the entire year — Tuesday morning, 10am, your biggest game ever.

It is Saturday. Three days before the final. At training today, your best player, Jordan, does not show up. No message. No explanation. Coach is frustrated but says nothing.

On the way home you bump into Jordan's younger sister. She goes quiet when you ask about Jordan. Then she says: "He said he's done with football. He won't say why."

Jordan is the reason you have a chance of winning. Without him your strategy falls apart. But something is clearly wrong.

The team needs to know what to do. You are the captain. You decide first.

🧠
Sports Psychology Fact
Sports psychologists work with athletes who have suddenly lost motivation or withdrawn from their sport. This is more common than most people think — and often has nothing to do with sport itself. The skill of noticing when a teammate has gone quiet is one of the most important in any team.
What do you do?

Scene 2 — The Final · 9:45am

Fifteen Minutes Before

The day of the final. Your team is in the changing room. Jordan is here — whatever happened this week, he showed up. He is quiet but he is here.

Your coach pulls you aside. "Look at them," she says. The team is tense. Everyone is tight and silent. Three players have been arguing since warm-up. The team that wins finals is loose and confident. This team looks like it is already losing.

Coach says: "You are the captain. You have fifteen minutes. I'll leave it to you."

Then she walks out of the changing room and closes the door.

🏆
What coaches actually do
Pre-match team psychology is one of the most researched areas in sports science. Studies show that how a team feels in the fifteen minutes before a game matters as much as how they have trained. Coaches, sports psychologists, and team captains all work on this — it is a real, learnable skill.
What do you do in those fifteen minutes?

Think like a coach.

The decisions you made in this simulation are the same ones coaches, sports psychologists, and team captains make every week. Let's think about what you chose and why.

Question 1
Jordan's sister said he was "done with football." Why might someone suddenly give up something they love — and what is the difference between "he needs space" and "he needs someone to check on him"?
Question 2
Your coach left the room and gave the fifteen minutes to you. Why do you think she did that rather than doing it herself?
Question 3
If you could have one conversation with Jordan before this whole week happened — when things were still normal — what would you have said or done differently?
