Scene 1

The Diagnosis

Something is wrong with your patient. You have 20 minutes to work out what.

You are a junior doctor. Four months into your first hospital placement at an NHS trust in Manchester. It is a Tuesday afternoon. Your senior doctor is in surgery and will not be reachable for the next ninety minutes.

A patient was admitted this morning: Mrs Nwosu, 67 years old. She came in complaining of tiredness and a mild headache. The nurses noted she seemed confused at times. Her initial blood tests came back thirty minutes ago. The nurse hands you the chart and says: "Something does not look right. Can you take a look?"

Patient Chart · Mrs Nwosu · F · 67 · Admitted 09:14
Blood pressure168/104 mmHg — HIGH
Blood sodium121 mmol/L — LOW (normal: 135–145)
Heart rate72 bpm — Normal
Temperature36.8°C — Normal
MedicationsRamipril (blood pressure). Started 3 weeks ago by GP.
Presenting symptomsFatigue, headache, mild confusion. No chest pain. No shortness of breath.

Low sodium is called hyponatremia. At this level, it can cause confusion, headaches — and if it drops further, seizures. The confusion Mrs Nwosu is experiencing could be coming from her sodium levels, not from a neurological problem. Or it could be both.

Your senior doctor is unreachable for 90 minutes. Mrs Nwosu's sodium is dangerously low. Her blood pressure is high. She was just started on a new blood pressure medication.

Scene 2 · 90 minutes later

The Conversation

Your senior doctor, Dr Okafor, has come out of surgery. You brief her on Mrs Nwosu. She listens carefully, reviews the chart, and nods.

"Good catch," she says. "Hyponatremia secondary to Ramipril. Classic presentation. We'll stop the medication and monitor. She should be fine in 48 hours."

Then she asks: "How did you feel handling that on your own?"

You tell her honestly. She listens. Then she says something that you will remember for the rest of your career.

"There are two kinds of junior doctors. The ones who wait to be taught what to think — and the ones who work through what they know, ask when they need help, and trust their clinical instinct while staying humble about its limits."

"Which one are you going to be? Because the answer to that question matters more than your grades."

Mrs Nwosu's family want to speak to you. They want to know what happened and what comes next. What do you say?

ProgressCase Notes
🩺

Think like a doctor.

Medicine is not memorising facts. It is making decisions under uncertainty with incomplete information, while caring about the person in front of you.

Question 01
Mrs Nwosu's confusion could have had several causes — low sodium, a neurological problem, or even dehydration. How do doctors decide what to investigate first when there are multiple possible explanations?
Question 02
Dr Okafor asked which kind of doctor you want to be — one who waits to be taught, or one who works through what they know. What is the difference in those two approaches, and which one do you think you are?
Question 03
Mrs Nwosu's family were worried and wanted answers. What do patients and families deserve to know — and when does "keeping it simple so they don't panic" become not giving them enough information to make their own decisions?
